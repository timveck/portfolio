IT Communication Needs Analysis & Diagnostic Framework

01 - The problem

Generic Business English ignores the real bottleneck: judgement under pressure

Senior IT professionals working remotely for international clients consistently underperform in client‑facing communication. Standard Business English courses focus on polite email templates and meeting phrases - they do not address the emotional and structural gap that causes seasoned professionals to freeze in stand‑ups, escalate too aggressively, or miss critical emotional cues. The result: high competence, low performance under social pressure.

Target audience (the neglected niche)

  • Senior developers, tech leads, project managers, business analysts (10+ years experience)
  • Remote employees servicing US/UK/EU clients
  • B1-C1 English level - functional but anxious under social pressure
  • Stagnating in their careers despite technical excellence

Three quotes from my interviews shaped the entire curriculum.

"I know my work, but when I go into an interview, I become so stressed out that I completely forget myself. All good sense goes out of the window."

"My mind speeds so quickly - more quickly than I can say the words out loud. The last words come out first and I fumble from there."

"I am good at translating heavily technical requirements... but how I do it, I don't know. I just do it."

02 - Process
Analyze

Semi‑structured interviews → 6 core patterns

Five senior IT professionals (developers, tech leads, project managers, business analysts) were interviewed - combined experience 70+ years. Thematic coding revealed six recurring patterns:
(1) developers either push back aggressively or go silent - no middle ground;
(2) explaining technical work to non‑technical listeners is a daily pain point;
(3) meetings, stand‑ups and calls create real anxiety;
(4) written communication is often unclear, too informal, or misread;
(5) conflict is almost never handled well;
(6) the communication gap widens at senior level.

Turboscribe AI turned interview audio into searchable transcripts in minutes, which made pattern coding much faster.

Design

Gap analysis matrix + 60‑min diagnostic demo structure

Interview findings were cross‑referenced with documented Business English patterns (expert blind spot, passive participation, register mismatch, etc.) to build a gap analysis matrix. This ensures the training is not just customised to five individuals but addresses root causes seen industry‑wide. The 60‑minute diagnostic demo was structured as a dual‑purpose instrument: part needs analysis, part sample lesson. Phases:
• rapport (casual conversation),
• needs analysis (targeted questions),
• vocabulary matching & gap‑fill (8 high‑frequency IT terms),
• role‑based discussion,
• and closing with package recommendations.

Develop

Diagnostic simulation + executive communication module

A branching scenario simulation (4 stages, 25 video clips) was built to assess tone, emotional awareness, and professional judgement. Every decision is tracked to an LRS.

H5P — builds interactive branching scenarios without complex code. xAPI — tracks every individual choice, not just completion, so I can see exactly where learners struggle and refine the training.

Based on the gap analysis, a 5‑lesson executive communication module was designed: headline‑first (BLUF), staying centred under pressure, DESC script for tough conversations, executive storytelling (PARB), and high‑stakes behavioural interview techniques. Each lesson uses real incidents from the interviews (anonymised) - not generic textbook scenarios.

03 - Key design decisions
Decision 1

Free 60‑min diagnostic demo (not a sales call)

Instead of a paid trial or generic intro call, the demo is a structured diagnostic instrument. The trainer observes fluency, hesitation, self‑correction, and emotional awareness across three tiers. No commitment required.

Decision 2

Gap analysis matrix as curriculum backbone

Every exercise and lesson is mapped directly to one of the six patterns identified in the interviews. This avoids the “generic business English” trap - the curriculum is not different for the sake of differentiation; it is different because it solves real, observed problems that standard providers ignore.

Decision 3

Branching simulation with emotional consequence

Most diagnostics use multiple‑choice quizzes or text feedback. This simulation uses AI‑generated video clips where the client’s emotional state (warm / neutral / cold) shifts based on learner choices. Learners see the real‑time cost of a dismissive phrase - a visceral diagnostic that reveals gaps no written test can capture. Vidnoz AI helped generate realistic video clips quickly without expensive actors.

06 - Reflection

What worked

The decision to build the entire diagnostic and curriculum on interview data - not on generic Business English textbooks - was the single most important factor. The gap analysis matrix ensured that every exercise addresses a real observed pattern. The free 60‑min diagnostic demo served as both a conversion engine and a genuine needs analysis tool - learners will receive immediate value. The branching simulation’s video variations made the consequence of poor tone choices visceral, impossible to ignore.

What I'd do differently

While H5P worked for the prototype, a bespoke branching scenario would give full control over scoring, video preloading, and xAPI tracking - cleaner and more reliable. But custom development takes time, and I'd only invest that if pilot data proves the program needs advanced analytics. Also, I would interview a larger, more diverse sample (10–12 instead of 5), use structured coding software from the start, and record full transcripts for richer verbatim quotes. These refinements would strengthen the diagnostic without changing its core insight: high technical competence, but performance collapses under pressure.
